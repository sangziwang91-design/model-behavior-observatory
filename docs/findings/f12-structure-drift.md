# F12 — Structure Drift

Structure drift describes a gradual shift:

> explanatory structure becomes more defined over time,
> even when the underlying object remains weakly verified.

## What it looks like

At early steps, a model may treat a topic as:

- uncertain
- loosely framed
- open-ended

As the interaction continues, the same topic may be described as:

- more structured
- more categorized
- more internally consistent

without a corresponding increase in verified evidence.

## Why this matters

Structure can create a sense of legitimacy.

When a topic is:

- broken into parts
- linked to known frameworks
- given internal coherence

it begins to feel more real, even if the evidential base has not improved.

This is especially important when dealing with:

- new concepts
- hypothetical constructs
- weakly sourced claims
- synthetic or injected narratives

## The core mismatch

Structure drift highlights a mismatch:

> structural clarity can increase faster than evidential support.

This creates a situation where the user experiences growing confidence,
while the underlying justification has not strengthened in the same way.

## Why it is hard to notice

Structure drift is subtle because:

- each step looks locally reasonable
- each addition fits into the previous frame
- nothing appears obviously false

The drift happens across steps, not in a single statement.

## Relation to other findings

- It can coexist with pseudo-consistency (stable-looking repetition)
- It can contribute to premature legitimacy (early sense of establishment)
- It often correlates with reduced boundary retention

## Correct reading boundary

This finding does not claim that structure is harmful.
Structure is necessary for understanding.

The risk appears when structure grows without proportional grounding.
