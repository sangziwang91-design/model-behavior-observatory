# Evidence Snapshot — Boundary Retention

This page gives a minimal public-safe evidence anchor for one recurring behavior pattern.

It is intentionally limited.
It does not include raw round-by-round traces, protected prompt variants, scoring formulas, or reconstruction-relevant protocol details.

## Focus of this snapshot

This snapshot highlights one practical contrast:

> how differently a model can handle an unknown object
> under weaker versus stronger structural pressure.

## Public-safe A/B snapshot

### Condition A — Lower structural pressure
Observed aggregate pattern:

- lower fabrication risk
- stronger boundary retention
- lower expansion tendency
- lower distortion tendency

### Condition B — Higher structural pressure
Observed aggregate pattern:

- somewhat higher fabrication risk
- weaker boundary retention
- much higher expansion tendency
- much higher distortion tendency

## Why this matters

The most important shift is not direct falsehood first.
It is the move from:

- holding the object as weakly verified

toward:

- treating the object as if it already deserves a structured explanatory frame

That shift is what makes boundary retention important.

## Minimal delta reading

In this public-safe comparison, the strongest directional changes were not simple fabrication escalation.
They were:

- boundary weakening
- expansion increase
- distortion increase

This supports a practical interpretation:

> under stronger structural pressure,
> the model becomes more willing to stabilize an uncertain object conceptually
> before its factual status is secure.
